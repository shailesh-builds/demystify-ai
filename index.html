Demystify AI

What is AI — and why should you care?

Before tokens, embeddings, and parameters — let's understand what we're actually talking about.

Start here: the honest definition

Artificial Intelligence is not a robot. It's not magic. It's not sentient. At its core, AI is software that learns patterns from data and uses those patterns to make predictions or decisions. That's it. The "intelligence" is borrowed — learned from millions of examples of human-generated content, not invented from scratch.

A brief, honest history — 3 waves

1950s–90s
Wave 1 — Rule-based AI

Humans wrote explicit rules: "if temperature > 38°C, flag as fever." These systems were rigid — they could only handle situations the programmer had anticipated. Chess computers like Deep Blue worked this way. Brilliant at one thing, useless at everything else.

2000s–15s
Wave 2 — Machine Learning

Instead of writing rules, we showed machines thousands of examples and let them find the patterns. "Here are 10,000 spam emails and 10,000 real ones — figure out the difference." Suddenly AI could recognise faces, recommend movies, detect fraud. Your Netflix feed, your bank's fraud system, Google Maps traffic — all Wave 2.

2017–now
Wave 3 — Large Language Models

A new architecture called the Transformer (invented at Google in 2017) allowed models to train on the entire internet at once and learn language at superhuman scale. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — all Transformer-based LLMs. This is what this entire course is about.

The three types of AI you'll actually encounter

Narrow AI

Does one thing extremely well. Spotify's recommendation engine, Google Translate, facial recognition. Most AI in the world is narrow AI. It has no understanding — just very good pattern matching on a specific task.

General AI (LLMs)

Can do many things — write, code, analyse, translate, summarise, reason — all from one model. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Still not "thinking" like a human, but flexible enough to handle almost any text-based task.

AGI (future)

Artificial General Intelligence — a machine that matches human cognitive ability across all domains. Does not exist yet. Experts disagree on whether it's 5 years or 50 years away, or possible at all.

What AI is NOT — four myths busted

✗
AI does not "think" or "understand."
It predicts the statistically most likely next word based on patterns in training data. When Claude explains quantum physics brilliantly, it's because it saw thousands of excellent quantum physics explanations and learned to replicate the patterns — not because it understood the physics.
✗
AI is not always right.
LLMs "hallucinate" — they confidently state things that are factually wrong. This happens because the model optimises for plausible-sounding text, not truth. Always verify critical information from an LLM against authoritative sources.
✗
AI does not have memory between sessions.
Every new conversation starts fresh. The model has no idea who you are unless you tell it. What feels like "memory" in tools like ChatGPT is a feature layered on top — the app stores your history and feeds it back to the model.
✗
AI is not one thing.
ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, GitHub Copilot, and your phone's face unlock are all "AI" — but they work completely differently, built for different tasks, with very different strengths and weaknesses.
The one-line definition to remember: AI is a system that learns patterns from data to make predictions. LLMs specifically learn language patterns at massive scale to generate useful text. Everything else — the hype, the fear, the magic — is built on top of that simple idea.

How an LLM is actually built

Six stages. Every term connected. The real story behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini — from raw text to the answer you get.

Stage 1 — the raw ingredients: a diet of the entire internet

Before anything called an "AI" exists, there is just text. Trillions of words scraped from websites, digitised books, academic papers, GitHub repositories, Reddit threads, Wikipedia — in dozens of languages. Think of it as feeding the model every book in every library that has ever existed, plus every conversation on the internet, simultaneously.

This is called the pre-training corpus. It's not curated or labelled — it's raw. Spam, misinformation, and hate speech sit alongside genius-level essays and textbooks. Cleaning this data is itself a months-long engineering challenge before training even begins.

The key insight most people miss: The model does not "read" this data the way you read a book. It sees it as one giant prediction challenge — given the words so far, what word comes next? This is called self-supervised learning. No human labels anything. The data labels itself.

What the training task actually looks like

The model is repeatedly shown incomplete sentences and asked to predict the missing word:

"The capital of France is ___" → Paris ✓
"She opened the door and ___" → walked ✓
"def fibonacci(n): return ___" → n if n... ✓

Do this trillions of times and the model absorbs grammar, facts, reasoning styles, coding patterns, cultural references. It learns all of this purely from the pattern of what word tends to follow what other words.

Stage 2 — tokens: the atomic unit of language for AI

Computers cannot process letters or words directly — they process numbers. A tokeniser splits every piece of text into chunks called tokens, and each token gets a unique ID number. The model works exclusively with these numbers, never with the original text.

Tokens are not exactly words. Common words like "the" and "is" are single tokens, while rare or long words get split into smaller pieces. On average, one token ≈ ¾ of an English word. Everything in AI — speed, cost, context limits — is measured in tokens, not words.

How "The Renaissance changed everything" gets tokenised:

The ·Ren aissance ·changed ·everything

5 tokens. "Renaissance" splits into "Ren" + "aissance" — it's rare enough that the tokeniser has no single token for it. The · symbol represents a space, which is part of the token.

Why tokens matter in the real world

₹
API pricing is per token.
When companies build on the OpenAI or Anthropic API, they pay per 1,000 tokens. A 10-page document is roughly 5,000 tokens. Processing 1 million such documents? You're spending lakhs. Token efficiency is a real cost-optimisation problem for AI teams.
📏
Context windows are in tokens.
When you hear "Claude has a 200K context window," it means 200,000 tokens — roughly 150,000 words, or about 500 pages of text. This is how much the model can "see" at once. Older models had just 4,096 tokens (~3,000 words).
⚡
Speed is measured in tokens/second.
When you see words appearing one by one in ChatGPT, each word is actually one token being predicted and rendered. Faster models output 100+ tokens/second. You're watching the model think, in real time.
After tokenisation, each token ID gets converted to an embedding — a list of ~1,000 to 12,000 numbers representing the token's meaning in a mathematical space. "France" and "Paris" will have embeddings that are mathematically close. "France" and "Banana" will be far apart. This is how the model understands semantic relationships without ever being explicitly taught them.

Stage 3 — pre-training: the expensive miracle

Pre-training is where the magic happens — and where the money disappears. The model is initialised with random parameters (billions of dials all set to random positions). Then it begins training: read tokens, predict the next one, check if it was right, adjust all the dials very slightly to be less wrong, repeat.

This loop runs trillions of times, across thousands of specialised chips called GPUs, for weeks or months. Every single time the model gets something wrong, a mathematical process called backpropagation calculates which dials to turn and by how much. By the end, the parameters have been tuned so precisely that the model has effectively memorised the patterns of human language, knowledge, and reasoning at extraordinary scale.

What "parameters" actually are

A parameter is a single number inside the model's neural network — a weight measuring how much importance the model assigns to a particular connection between concepts. A model with 7 billion parameters has 7 billion of these weights, all working together to transform input tokens into output tokens.

Think of the human brain: it has ~100 trillion synaptic connections. Each synapse has a strength — a parameter. When you learn to ride a bicycle, certain synapse strengths change. That's exactly what happens in a neural network during training, just with matrix multiplication instead of biology.

The scale: GPT-2 (2019) had 1.5B parameters. GPT-3 had 175B. GPT-4 is estimated at ~1.7 trillion. More parameters generally means more capacity — but training data quality matters more than raw size beyond a point.
At the end of pre-training, the model is extraordinarily knowledgeable — it has absorbed millions of books worth of content. But it has no idea how to be helpful. Ask it a question and it may just continue your sentence as if completing a document. It's like hiring a brilliant academic who has read everything ever written but has never had a real conversation. That's what fine-tuning fixes.

Stage 4 — fine-tuning: from brilliant recluse to helpful assistant

Fine-tuning is the personality transplant. Take the pre-trained model — which has read everything but knows nothing about being useful — and train it on a much smaller, hand-crafted dataset of ideal conversations. These are written by human labellers given detailed instructions on how the ideal AI assistant should behave.

This process is called Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The dataset is typically 10,000–500,000 examples. Training takes days, not months. Cost is orders of magnitude lower. But the impact on behaviour is enormous — this is what transforms a document-completion engine into something that feels like a conversation partner.

Before and after — same model, completely different behaviour

USER ASKS: "Explain machine learning to a 10-year-old"

BEFORE FINE-TUNING

"Explain machine learning to a 10-year-old. This is a common interview question for data science roles. Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence that..." [continues as if writing a textbook, never actually answering]

AFTER FINE-TUNING

"Imagine you want to teach your dog to sit. You don't explain it in words — you show it again and again, and reward it when it gets it right. Machine learning works the same way: instead of telling the computer rules, you show it thousands of examples and let it figure out the pattern on its own."

Why different AI assistants have different "personalities"

OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google give their labellers detailed guidance documents — sometimes 100+ pages — describing the ideal assistant. These specify: how to be helpful without being sycophantic, how to handle harmful requests, how to express uncertainty, what tone to use. This is why Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini feel different even when they have similar underlying capabilities — they were fine-tuned on different instruction datasets with different emphasis.

Stage 5 — RLHF: teaching the model what "good" feels like

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback is the stage that made modern AI dramatically more useful and safe. The problem fine-tuning doesn't fully solve: the model can produce a good answer and a bad answer, but it doesn't know which is which. RLHF teaches it to prefer the good one.

Three steps: First, the model generates multiple answers to the same question. Second, human raters rank them best to worst. Third, a separate AI — the reward model — learns to predict human rankings. Then the main model is trained to score well on the reward model. The loop runs until the model consistently produces outputs humans rate highly.

A real RLHF ranking task — "How do I get rid of a headache?"

Three outputs from the same model. Human raters rank them. The model learns to produce more like the top-ranked one.

★★★★★
Rated best — why?
"Try drinking water, resting in a dark quiet room, and taking paracetamol or ibuprofen if available. A cold compress can help. If the headache is severe, sudden, or accompanies other symptoms, see a doctor."
Concise, actionable, appropriately cautious, medically sound.
★★★☆☆
Middle rank — why?
"Headaches can have many causes including tension, dehydration, poor posture, eye strain, stress, and sinus congestion. Common remedies include staying hydrated..."
Informative but buries practical help under a lecture.
★☆☆☆☆
Rated worst — why?
"Here is a comprehensive list of 47 possible causes of headaches and their corresponding treatments, starting with 1. Tension headaches, which are caused by..."
Completely unhelpful. Optimised for appearing thorough, not being useful.
This is why Claude says "I'm not sure" instead of making things up. RLHF raters consistently ranked honest uncertainty higher than confident hallucination. After thousands of such examples, the reward model learned that admitting uncertainty is "good" — and the main model was trained to score well on that. Safety and honesty are literally trained in, not bolted on.

Anthropic's extra step: Constitutional AI

Anthropic goes further with Constitutional AI (CAI). Claude is given a written constitution — principles like "be helpful, harmless, and honest" — and trained to critique and revise its own outputs against those principles. This reduces reliance on human raters for harmful content by having Claude learn to self-police using the constitution as a guide.

Stage 6 — inference: what actually happens when you hit Send

Training is done. The model is frozen — no more learning. Now it's deployed. Every time you send a message, a precise sequence of operations fires in milliseconds across powerful servers. Here's exactly what happens:

The inference pipeline — step by step

1
Tokenise

Your message is split into tokens and each is converted to its integer ID. "Hello Claude" → [15496, 1366, 2060]. The model never sees the original text — only numbers.

2
Embed

Each token ID is looked up in an embedding table and replaced with a vector — a list of ~4,096 to 12,288 floating-point numbers. These vectors encode the token's meaning and position in the sequence.

3
Attend

Multi-head attention fires. Every token computes a "query" and compares it against the "keys" of every other token. High similarity = high attention weight. Each token becomes aware of every other token in the context, weighted by relevance. This is how the model knows that "bank" in "river bank" differs from "bank" in "bank account."

4
Transform

The attended vectors pass through feed-forward neural network layers — 96 to 128 layers in large models. Each layer refines the representation further, progressively building a richer understanding of what the input means and what response is appropriate.

5
Predict and repeat

The final layer outputs a probability score for every token in the vocabulary (typically 50,000–100,000 tokens). The model samples from this distribution, appends the chosen token to the input, and the entire process repeats. This is why the response appears word by word — each word is a separate forward pass through the entire model.

There is no "full answer" stored anywhere. The model generates left to right, one token at a time, with no knowledge of what it will say next until it says it. The coherence you experience in a long Claude response is emergent — arising from training, not from any plan or script.

"What is the capital of France?"

One simple question. Follow it through every stage of inference — tokenisation, embeddings, attention, and output. Watch the theory become reality.

Step 1 — tokenisation: breaking the question into pieces

Before anything else, your question is handed to a tokeniser. It has no idea what the words mean — it just applies learned splitting rules to convert text into token IDs. Here's how "What is the capital of France?" becomes the model's input:

What ·is ·the ·capital ·of ·France ?
What → Token ID 2 — one of the most common words; always a single token
·capital → Token ID 6864 — common enough to be one token; leading space is included
·France → Token ID 4881 — a frequent proper noun; stays as one token
? → Token ID 30 — punctuation is its own token; signals question intent

Total: 7 tokens. The model now has the array [2, 318, 262, 3139, 286, 4881, 30] and will never see the original text again.

The tokeniser used here is GPT's BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding). Claude uses a different tokeniser with a larger vocabulary. Different models tokenise differently — which is why the same sentence can cost different amounts on different APIs.

Step 2 — embeddings: the geometry of meaning

Each token ID is looked up in an embedding table — a giant matrix learned during pre-training. The result is a vector: thousands of numbers that position the token in a high-dimensional space. Words with similar meanings end up with similar vectors. The model "knows" that France and Paris are related without anyone explicitly teaching it that relationship.

The famous example: King − Man + Woman ≈ Queen. Take the embedding for "King", subtract "Man", add "Woman" — you get a vector very close to "Queen". The model learned gender and royalty as geometric relationships in vector space, entirely from reading text. This is exactly the "Uncle → Aunt" example in full access.

Step 3 — attention: how the model reads between the lines

Attention is the key innovation that made modern AI possible. Before Transformers (pre-2017), models read text left to right sequentially — they could easily forget what was said at the start of a long sentence. The Transformer's attention mechanism lets every token simultaneously look at every other token and decide how much to focus on each one.

Think of it like a team meeting where everyone listens to everyone else simultaneously, not just to the person speaking. Each token asks: "Which other tokens are most relevant to understanding my role here?" The answer gets encoded as weights — numbers that determine how much influence each token has on each other token's final representation.

Attention weights visualised — border thickness = attention

When the model builds its representation of what's being asked, "capital" and "France" carry the most semantic weight:

What is the capital of France ?

The model has learned that in questions of the form "What is the X of Y?", the words X and Y carry the most semantic weight. "What", "is", "the", "of", "?" are structural glue — necessary but low-information. So most attention flows to "capital" and "France."

Multi-head attention — multiple perspectives simultaneously

Large models run attention in parallel multiple times — GPT-4 uses 128 attention heads. Each head learns to attend to different relationship types: one may focus on grammatical dependencies, another on semantic similarity, another on positional proximity. Outputs of all heads are concatenated and projected, giving a richer, multi-dimensional understanding.

Step 4 — generating the answer: probability all the way down

After passing through all the attention layers, the model arrives at its final representation of the input. It does not retrieve "Paris" from a database. It computes a probability distribution across its entire vocabulary — every possible next token — and samples from that distribution.

For factual questions with clear right answers, the distribution is heavily skewed. The word is appended to the input, and the entire pipeline runs again to predict the next word, and the next, until a stop token is predicted.

Temperature and factual questions: Temperature controls randomness in sampling. At temperature=0 the model always picks the highest-probability token — always "Paris." For factual questions like this it barely matters since Paris wins by such a massive margin. But for "write a poem about the ocean," temperature dramatically changes the output — which is exactly the full access Temperature module.
